What Is Ethical Hacking?

Ethical hacking is like being a digital superhero with a legal license. You legally hack systems, not to harm, but to protect. These professionals, called white-hat hackers, break into computers with permission to find and fix weaknesses before the bad guys (black-hat hackers) do.

What Are The Types Of Ethical Hacking ?

Each type of ethical hacking is a special mission targeting a specific area of technology.

Here are the most important ones:

  1. Network Hacking

    • Definition:

      This is like checking if someone can sneak into your house through hidden tunnels. Ethical hackers test computer networks, routers, and Wi-Fi to find weak spots.

      They ask: “Can someone get inside this system without permission?”

    • Where it's used:Offices, banks, schools , ariports
      •  
    • Salary:$50,000-$150,000 per year
      •  
    • Tool: Nmap, Wireshark, Nessus
      •  
    • Example: Testing a company’s Wi-Fi to see if the password is easy to crack.
      •  


  2. Web Application Hacking

    • Definition:

      • This is like testing the locks and windows of a website. Hackers look for bugs in websites and apps, like login forms or shopping carts.

      They ask: “Can I get in and see something I shouldn’t?”

    • Where it's used:E-commerce, banking apps, blogs
      •  
    • Bug Bounty Range:$100 to $100,000 per bug
      •  
    • Tool: Burp Suite, OWASP ZAP
      •  
    • Example: Trying to log in as admin without a password by tricking the site’s code.
      •  


  3. Wireless Hacking

    • Definition:

      • This is like standing outside a building and checking if the Wi-Fi doors are open. Hackers test wireless networks like Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and smart devices.

      They ask: “Can someone from outside break into your internet?”

    • Where it's used:Public Wi-Fi spots, hotels, schools
      •  
    • Freelancer Pay :$500 to $2,000 per job
      •  
    • Tool: Aircrack-ng, Kismet
      •  
    • Example: Capturing Wi-Fi signals to check if they can be cracked.
      •  


  4. Social Engineering

    • Definition:

      • This is not about hacking computers, but hacking people. It’s like pretending to be someone else to trick others into giving passwords or secrets.

      They ask: “Can I fool someone into trusting me enough to give access?”

    • Where it's used:Company security awareness programs
      •  
    • Salary:: Up to $80,000/year
      •  
    • Methods: Phishing , fake call, fake emails
      •  
    • Example: Sending a fake email that looks like it’s from the boss asking for login details.
      •  


  5. System Hackingg

    • Definition:

      • This is like testing if you can break into a locked computer using secret codes or viruses. Hackers try to access files, passwords, or software.

      They ask: “Can I take control of this computer without anyone knowing?”

    • Where it's used:IT companies, government systems
      •  
    • Salary: $60,000–$130,000
      •  
    • Tools: Metasploit, John the Ripper, Mimikatz
      •  
    • Example:Running a password cracker to break into an old system.
      •  


  6. Cloud Hacking

    • Definition:

      This is like checking if a digital storage locker (like Google Drive or AWS) has holes in it. Hackers test cloud servers to see if anyone left the door open.

      • They ask: “Is your cloud storage safe from the outside world?”

    • Where it's used:Tech companies, startups, government
      •  
    • Salary:$90,000–$180,000
      •  
    • Tools: ScoutSuite, Prowler
      •  
    • Example: Finding passwords or keys stored publicly by mistake.
      •  


  7. Mobile App Hacking

    • Definition:

      • This is like checking if a phone app is leaking secrets or letting someone else peek inside. Hackers test apps to see how they store and send data.

      They ask: “Can someone break this app and steal user data?”

    • Where it's used:Android/iOS app developers
      •  
    • Salary: $70,000–$140,000/year
      •  
    • Tools: MobSF, Frida, APKTool
      •  
    • Example: Checking if a banking app sends your password without encryption.
      •  


  8. Physical Hacking

    • Definition:

      • This is real-life hacking—trying to sneak into buildings, server rooms, or computers without using digital tools.

      They ask: “Can someone get inside physically and plug in a USB or steal a laptop?”

    • Where it's used:Government offices, data centers
      •  
    • Salary:$90,000–$200,000/year
      •  
    • Method: Fake badges, tailgating, hidden USB devices
      •  
    • Example: Pretending to be a cleaner to access restricted areas.
      •  



    What Are The Types Of Ethical Hackers?

    There are many kinds of ethical hackers—each with a unique style, like characters in a cyber-adventure story. Some are guardians, some are bounty hunters, some are undercover agents. Let’s meet them:

    White Hat Hacker

    White-hat hacker walk the line of good. They always follow the rules, hack with permission, and work to protect people and systems.

     

    They are hired by companies, schools, governments—even hospitals—to test for weaknesses inwebsites, apps, or networks.

     

    “Can I safely break into your system so I can help fix it before a bad guy does?”

     

    Where They Work: Cybersecurity firms, government agencies, big tech companies

     

    How much they earn: $50,000 to $150,000+ per year

     

    Risk: None. They work legally and are often highly respected in the industry.

    Gray Hat Hacker

    They hack without permission, but they usually don’t mean to hurt anyone. Instead, they might try to help by exposing flaws—even if they didn’t ask first

     

    They’re like rogue agents—not evil, but not always playing by the rules. Sometimes they report the bugs for free; sometimes they ask for a reward.

     

    “I found a hole in your system. I didn’t break anything—but you should fix it.”

     

    Where They Operate: Online communities, tech forums, bug bounty platforms

     

    How much they earn: Sometimes nothing, sometimes thousands per vulnerability

     

    Risk: Medium. Even if they mean well, hacking without permission is still illegal in many places.

    Black Hat Hacker

    They break the rules, hack without permission, and often do it to steal, destroy, or cause harm.

     

    They might target websites, banks, companies—or even regular people—to take money, data, or control.

     

    “How can I break into this system and take something valuable—without getting caught?”

     

    They’re like cyber thieves, hiding in the shadows, always looking for a weak spot to exploit.

     

    Where They Operate:Secret underground forums, dark web, illegal cybercrime groups

     

    How much they earn:It varies—some make nothing, others steal millions

     

    Risk:Very high. If caught, they can face heavy fines or years in prison.
